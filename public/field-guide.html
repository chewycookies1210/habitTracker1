Part 1

Why this works differently than "try harder"

01

Action comes before motivation

Waiting to feel ready keeps the loop going. BJ Fogg's research on small habits shows the fix is shrinking the first step so far it doesn't need motivation at all.

02

If-then plans beat willpower

Psychologist Peter Gollwitzer's studies on implementation intentions found specific "if X, then I do Y" plans get followed through far more than vague goals like "study more."

03

Environment beats self-control

Willpower is unreliable and fades across the day. Friction is more reliable — make the good option one step closer, the bad option a few steps further.

04

Habits stack onto existing routines

A new habit sticks better anchored to something already stable — "after I close my laptop, I put my phone in the other room" — than left floating and easy to forget.

05

Self-compassion beats self-criticism

Kristin Neff's research on self-compassion found harsh self-talk after a lapse predicts more repetition, not less. Noticing and resetting works better than punishing.

06

Scrolling and adult content share a mechanism

Both exploit variable-reward loops — the same unpredictable-timing mechanism that makes slot machines compelling. The fix is reducing entry points, not fighting it in the moment.

Part 2

Tactics for the three habits you named

Trigger: boredom, avoidance

Instagram

Add friction

Log out after every use, delete the app from the home screen, set a hard daily timer that locks instead of "remind me."

Bounded window

One or two intentional windows a day (built into the schedule) rather than an all-or-nothing ban — bounded permission reduces the pull the rest of the day.

Replacement

Keep your phone in another room during work/study blocks; keep a book or your gym bag visible as the easier reach.

Trigger: stress, habit cue

Smoking

Add friction

Buy one at a time, not a fresh pack when low. Keep it in a bag or car, not your pocket.

If-then plan

"If I want one after a stressful call, I take a 5-minute walk first, then decide." Delay is often enough to break the automatic loop.

Bigger step later

When ready to reduce or quit fully, loop in your doctor or psychologist — structured support outperforms solo willpower here.

Trigger: loneliness, downtime

Adult content

Add friction

A site/app blocker (e.g. a content filter or app like Cold Turkey) set up on a motivated day, so it's already there on the days you're not.

Name the trigger

Loneliness, boredom, and procrastination-avoidance are the most common drivers — match the response to the actual trigger (call someone vs. start the task you're avoiding).

No self-punishment

Track lapses as data, not failure. Bring the pattern to your psychologist once you have a couple weeks of real frequency/trigger notes.

Four triggers show up across all three habits: boredom, stress, loneliness, procrastination-avoidance. Naming which one is driving a given urge is what makes the matched response actually work.

Training program (full body, 3x/week)

Based on InBody scan (07.06.2026): 72.6kg, 15.6% body fat, 34.8kg skeletal muscle mass, InBody score 86. Full-body was chosen over a split because at 3 sessions/week, each muscle needs 2x/week frequency for best hypertrophy — a 3-day split only hits each muscle once. Rotate A/B/C each session. Rest 2–3 min on compounds, 60–90s on accessories.
